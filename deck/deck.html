Hard constraints

And the dataset fights back

It is deliberately seeded with traps: keyword stuffers (off-domain people with perfect AI skill lists), ~80 honeypots with impossible profiles (>10% in your top 100 = disqualified), and stale “perfect-on-paper” candidates who aren’t actually reachable.

The challenge02
The insight that shaped everything

This is a profile-reasoning problem, not a search problem

The organizers say it outright: “the right answer is not the candidate with the most AI keywords — that’s a trap we built in.” A single resume-vs-JD similarity score amplifies every trap.

What loses

  • Cosine / embedding similarity → ranks the keyword stuffers up
  • Skill-list matching → skills are pure noise (next slide)
  • An LLM per candidate → forbidden, and won’t fit 5 min on CPU

What wins

  • Read the career history: did they actually build ranking / retrieval / recsys in production?
  • Encode the JD’s real rubric as transparent, auditable rules
  • Weigh availability — unreachable ≠ hireable
Reframing the problem03
Evidence → decisions

We let the 100K profiles decide the architecture

Skills are noise

Every skill appears on ~12% of candidates, near-uniform across a huge vocabulary. A Marketing Manager is as likely to list “Kafka” as an engineer.

→ Never reward bare skill presence.

No hidden “Tier-5” gems

All 98,821 non-ML-titled candidates have zero retrieval/ranking evidence in their career text.

→ Embeddings add nothing. Dropped them — faster, simpler, trap-proof.

Honeypots are impossible

e.g. a skill used 92 months on a 3-year career; “expert” in 10 skills with 0 months; an 8-yr role spanning 16 months of dates.

→ Catch them by numeric consistency, not by guessing.

Where the real signal lives

Career descriptions separate the real from the fake: genuine ML engineers describe ranking, retrieval, recommendation and search work; stuffers list AI skills but their role descriptions are about sales, accounting, or design.

What the data told us04
Architecture

A five-stage, fully transparent pipeline

No LLM, no embeddings, no GPU in the ranking step — every score is explainable line by line.

STAGE 0

Normalize

Parse 100K profiles; derive tenure, career text, skill grounding, summary-vs-profile YoE.

STAGE 1

Honeypot gate

Force impossible profiles to tier 0 via numeric inconsistency.

STAGE 2

Role-fit gate

Is this an AI/ML production engineer? Title × career × evidence.

STAGE 3

Multi-dim score

Weighted JD rubric + disqualifier down-weights.

STAGE 4

Availability

Multiplier from behavioral signals.

Stage 5 — Rank & explain

Sort (score desc, id asc), take top 100, generate grounded per-candidate reasoning, emit validator-clean CSV.

One config file

Every weight, keyword family, disqualifier rule and threshold lives in redrob/rubric.py — the recruiter’s mental model, encoded.

System architecture05
The decisive trap defense · Stage 2

Why the keyword stuffer collapses

Candidate: “Marketing Manager”

Headline: “AI enthusiast | Building with LLMs.” Skills: RAG, Embeddings, Fine-tuning LLMs, LangChain.

But every career stint is sales, brand design, accounting — and the summary admits “taking online courses on RAG.”

role_fit ≈ 0.05 → ranked #27 of 68, score 0.058

How the gate scores it

role_fit = title trajectory × career ML-ness × production-evidence in descriptions.

Skills never enter. An off-domain title with no ML career evidence cannot be rescued by any skill list.

Guarded disqualifiers (so honest framing isn’t over-punished):

dabblerpure-researchconsulting-onlyperception-onlytitle-chaserbig-tech-only
Role-fit gate06
Stages 3 & 4

Explainable dimensions, then an availability reality-check

Stage 3 — weighted fit (JD rubric)

  • role-fit (34%) · production-evidence (24%)
  • experience band 5–9 yr (12%) · trajectory (10%)
  • domain NLP/IR match (10%) · location (10%)

Each dimension is in [0,1] and reported per candidate — the ranking is auditable, not asserted.

Stage 4 — availability multiplier

The JD: “perfect on paper but hasn’t logged in for 6 months with a 5% response rate is not actually available.”

A multiplier (≈0.5–1.08) from response rate, recency, open-to-work, notice period, GitHub activity. It modulates fit — it never manufactures it.

Effect: a Microsoft NLP engineer with a 7% response rate and an 8-month-stale login drops out of the top 30.

Results08
Proof, not promise

Every trap is demonstrably handled

Honeypots

All known impossible profiles forced to tier 0 (score −1.0). A self-test asserts 0% in the top 100 on every run — well under the 10% disqualification line.

Keyword stuffers

The Marketing Manager with a perfect AI skill list lands at #27 / 0.058 — suppressed by the role-fit gate, not by a special case.

Stale & off-target

Unreachable candidates down-weighted; pure big-tech careers nudged toward the cutoff, per the JD’s preference for product/startup builders.

Built-in self-test

Asserts: known honeypots flagged · known strong fits preserved · 0% honeypots in top 100 · score monotonic by rank · ties broken by candidate_id. Runs against the full pool.

Trap handling09
Built to survive Stages 3–5

Honest, reproducible, and defensible

No fabricated anything

No ground truth ships with the data, so we report methodology and ablations — never a made-up NDCG. No fake confidence scores. No hallucinated skills in any reasoning string.

No LLM in the loop

The ranking step makes zero network/API calls and uses no GPU. An LLM (Claude) was used only in development to analyze data and shape the rules — declared honestly.

Trivial reproduction

Pure standard library — nothing to install, no version drift. One command regenerates the exact submission.

python rank.py --candidates candidates.jsonl --out submission.csv

Defensible by design

Every decision traces to a line in rubric.py and a fact in the data — ready to walk through in the engineering interview.
